What's Next for the NYC Real Estate Market?

The short answer: We just don't know!

 
Prior to the government imposed stay-at-home mandate in late March, the NYC real estate market had been experiencing an uptick in contract and closing activity as sellers continued to meet buyer demands with price discounts to close deals, especially in the below $3M price segment.
 
Not surprisingly, new listings and transaction activity have now all but dissipated. The initial plunge in the Dow and ongoing stock market volatility have only added to the assault. With so much uncertainty and the likelihood of a global recession even after we return to work, it's hard to predict when we will see a rebound in real estate activity. However, past history has demonstrated NYC's resiliency and ability to bounce back following both 9/11 and the 2007-2009 subprime mortgage crisis after the collapse of Lehman Brothers.
 
In the near term, we can anticipate a dislocated, thinly traded market. It will likely take several months for some degree of price rediscovery among sellers and buyers. It will be important, during this time, to closely monitor contract activity and sale prices. Pent-up demand over the lockdown may initiate a spark and accelerate the market forward. Once there is both seller and buyer confidence in our "new normal" and signs of improvement in the overall economy, we will hopefully see a swift comeback. We can only hope for this ideal outcome!

1Q 2020 Manhattan Market Report
January sales were close to last year’s while February had 10% more sales than in 2019. However, March performance marks the downturn of the market. The first week of March was hopeful with 8% more contracts signed, but this boost did not carry through the remainder of the month. 
 
As the severity of the COVID-19 outbreak in the area increased, activity in the market sharply declined. Contracts plummeted 42% year-over-year, 37% of active listings saw discounts and new inventory was almost nonexistent. Like the virus, the state of the real estate market remains in uncharted territory.

1Q 2020 Brooklyn Market Report
In Brooklyn, new contracts and sales activity came to a near-standstill at the tail end of Q1 2020. Year-over-year signed contracts decreased 11% in Q1 and could have exceeded 2019, except for the extreme softness in the market beginning in March. 
 
A positive indicator is that the average sales price increased 9% and the median price rose 8%. An additional sign of strength is that the average sale price per square foot in Brooklyn remained equal to that of Q1 2019. During Q1, various price points had differing strengths, indicating a fluid market. 
 
The final several weeks of March reflected the full impact of the NY State mandated stay-at-home orders, resulting in softer residential activity. Compared to Q1 2019, signed contracts were down 44%, new sales stalled and new listings were o 75%. The luxury property market, $2M and above, slowed as well. This price category had 15-20 new contracts each week in the first two months of the quarter, but late March weekly activity was in the single digits.
